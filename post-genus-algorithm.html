An Efficient Genus Algorithm Based on Graph Rotations

View on arXiv → Download PDF

Say you have three houses and three utilities, and you must connect each house to each utility with a wire, is there a way to do it so that none of the wires cross? In graph-theoretic terms: is K3,3K_{3,3}K3,3​ planar? Kuratowski's theorem tells us it isn't. But K3,3K_{3,3}K3,3​ is toroidal. It can be embedded on a torus without any edges crossing.

The property of a torus that lets us embed K3,3K_{3,3}K3,3​ is that it has a handle, unlike a plane or a sphere. This motivates classifying surfaces by their number of handles, the genus ggg. The minimum-genus surface for K3,3K_{3,3}K3,3​ has g=1g=1g=1, and we say K3,3K_{3,3}K3,3​ has genus 111.

For complete and complete bipartite graphs the genus is known in closed form, thanks to Ringel and Youngs:

g(Kn)=⌈(n−3)(n−4)12⌉,g(Km,n)=⌈(n−2)(m−2)4⌉.g(K_n)=\left\lceil\frac{(n-3)(n-4)}{12}\right\rceil,\qquad g(K_{m,n})=\left\lceil\frac{(n-2)(m-2)}{4}\right\rceil.g(Kn​)=⌈12(n−3)(n−4)​⌉,g(Km,n​)=⌈4(n−2)(m−2)​⌉.

But for an arbitrary graph it is not so simple. There are graphs whose genus is still unknown, and computing it in general is hard, in large part because of the difficulty of placing "bridges."

How PAGE works

With Alexander Metzger, we present PAGE, a Practical Algorithm for Graph Embedding. It works directly with rotation systems: at each vertex you record the cyclic (clockwise) order of its incident edges, and that combinatorial data alone encodes an embedding, with no picture of a surface required.

From a rotation system you trace out facial walks, stepping from each directed edge to the next one in the rotation, and these recover the faces of the embedding. If the embedding has FFF faces, Euler's formula fixes the genus:

n−m+F=2−2g⟹g=−n+m−F+22,n-m+F = 2-2g \quad\Longrightarrow\quad g=\frac{-n+m-F+2}{2},n−m+F=2−2g⟹g=2−n+m−F+2​,

so the minimum genus is attained exactly when the rotation system produces the most facial walks. PAGE searches for that rotation system efficiently, building up collections of closed facial walks and narrowing both the upper and lower bounds as it runs. This is how it sidesteps the bridge-placement difficulties that trip up many other genus algorithms. It is simple to implement, outputs the faces of an optimal embedding, and handles K7K_7K7​ and K8K_8K8​ in a few seconds, scaling to graphs with over a hundred edges in a few minutes. Its runtime is

O ⁣(n (4m/n) n/t) steps\mathcal{O}\!\left(n\,(4m/n)^{\,n/t}\right)\ \text{steps}O(n(4m/n)n/t) steps

for a graph with nnn vertices, mmm edges, and girth ttt. We used PAGE to settle genera that were previously unknown, most notably the (3,12)(3,12)(3,12)-cage, which turns out to have genus 171717. It also gave the first bounds for graphs still out of reach, like the (6,12)(6,12)(6,12)-cage and families of Johnson, Kneser, and cyclotomic graphs. The implementation is available under an MIT license.

The full paper develops the rotation-system machinery, the algorithm itself, and the worked examples in detail.

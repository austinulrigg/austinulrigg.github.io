Honors thesis · University of Washington · 2025

Graph Genera and Minors

Download the thesis (PDF) → UW ResearchWorks

My undergraduate honors thesis, advised by François Clément, is about embedding graphs on surfaces, with an emphasis on genus, rotation systems, and forbidden minors.

During World War II the Hungarian mathematician Pál Turán was made to push carts of bricks from kilns to storage sites. The hardest part of the job, he said, was when two rail tracks crossed: at each crossing he had to lift the heavy cart off the track and set it back down on the other side. He wondered whether the tracks could have been laid to minimize those crossings. Abstracting the kilns and sites as vertices and the tracks as edges, the question becomes: given a graph GGG, what is the fewest crossings in any drawing of it? That minimum is the crossing number cr(G)\mathrm{cr}(G)cr(G).

In the real world you avoid a crossing by separating the routes, with a bridge or a tunnel. Mathematically that is the same as drawing the graph on a different surface: one built from the sphere by adding handles.

Genus and surfaces

A surface of genus ggg is, up to homeomorphism, a sphere with ggg handles, equivalently ggg tori glued together, written SgS_gSg​. The classification of surfaces says that every compact surface without boundary is exactly one of these (or one of the non-orientable surfaces), for a uniquely determined genus, so genus is a fundamental invariant. The genus of a graph GGG is then the smallest ggg for which GGG embeds on SgS_gSg​ with no edges crossing: genus 000 is planar, genus 111 is toroidal.

Rotation systems

Reasoning about drawings on a surface directly is awkward, so the thesis leans on a combinatorial encoding. Around each vertex vvv of a cellular embedding, list the incident edges in clockwise order; the resulting cyclic permutation πv\pi_vπv​ is the local rotation at vvv, and the whole collection π={πv∣v∈V(G)}\pi=\{\pi_v \mid v\in V(G)\}π={πv​∣v∈V(G)} is the rotation system of the embedding.

The key fact (the Heffter–Edmonds–Ringel theorem) is that the rotation system determines the embedding up to homeomorphism, and conversely every rotation system defines a cellular embedding on some orientable surface. Using darts (directed half-edges) you can trace out the faces of that embedding, and once you know the number of vertices, edges, and faces, Euler's formula

V−E+F=2−2gV - E + F = 2 - 2gV−E+F=2−2g

hands you the genus. So genus can be computed directly from the rotation system, without ever drawing the graph on a surface.

Minors, and why this is hard

A graph is planar exactly when it contains neither K5K_5K5​ nor K3,3K_{3,3}K3,3​ as a minor, by Kuratowski's theorem in Wagner's form. So the planar graphs are pinned down by a finite list of forbidden minors.

Genus behaves well under this operation: if GGG embeds on SgS_gSg​, so does every minor of GGG, so for each fixed genus the embeddable graphs form a minor-closed family. The Robertson–Seymour graph minor theorem then guarantees that every such family has a finite list of forbidden minors. It is a staggering result. Its proof spans some twenty papers written from 1983 to 2004, over 500 pages, and it is non-constructive, promising the lists exist without telling us what they are.

Even computing the genus of a single graph can be brutal. Conceptually the question is simple, but confronted with something like the Tutte (3,12)(3,12)(3,12)-cage the difficulty becomes clear:

It has genus 171717, but to prove that one must (1) show it cannot embed on S16S_{16}S16​ and (2) construct an explicit embedding on S17S_{17}S17​, both highly non-trivial and almost hopeless by hand. That is exactly what pushes us toward rotation systems and an algorithm.

PAGE

The naive idea is to search over all rotation systems, trace the faces of each, and read off the smallest genus, but the number of rotation systems is astronomically large, so brute force collapses quickly. PAGE, the algorithm I developed with Alexander Metzger, makes the search practical: it incrementally builds up collections of closed facial walks and narrows upper and lower bounds on the genus as it runs, in

O ⁣(n (4m/n) n/t) steps\mathcal{O}\!\left(n\,(4m/n)^{\,n/t}\right)\ \text{steps}O(n(4m/n)n/t) steps

for a graph with nnn vertices, mmm edges, and girth ttt. We used it to settle the (3,12)(3,12)(3,12)-cage at genus 171717, and to give the first bounds on graphs whose genus is still unknown, like the (6,12)(6,12)(6,12)-cage and families of Johnson, Kneser, and cyclotomic graphs.

Forbidden toroidal minors, and open problems

The last part of the thesis returns to forbidden minors, for the simplest interesting surface: the torus. By Robertson–Seymour the toroidal graphs have a finite obstruction set F(S1)\mathcal{F}(S_1)F(S1​), but unlike the planar case, where the list is just K5K_5K5​ and K3,3K_{3,3}K3,3​, the full toroidal list is still unknown, and runs to thousands of graphs. I survey what is known, organize the obstructions, and discuss directions toward completing the set, including partitioning F(S1)\mathcal{F}(S_1)F(S1​) by connectivity. I close with open problems, among them pinning down the genus of the Balaban (3,11)(3,11)(3,11)-cage, which remains out of reach even for PAGE.

The full thesis develops all of this carefully, with the topological prerequisites collected in an appendix.
